Bayes' Theorem

Bayes' theorem is the mathematical backbone of our Titanic predictor. First described by Reverend Thomas Bayes, it gives us a way to update our beliefs when we observe new evidence — exactly what we do when we learn a passenger was female, 1st class, and 28 years old.

The Fundamental Theorem
P(A|B)  =  P(B|A)  ·  P(A) P(B)

01
Prior Probability
Our initial belief before seeing any evidence. In the Titanic dataset, 38.4% of passengers survived — so P(Survived) = 0.384. This is the prior our model starts with before looking at any individual features.
02
Likelihood
How probable is the observed data given a class? For Gaussian NB, each feature follows a normal distribution. P(Female | Survived) is much higher than P(Female | Died) because women had priority access to lifeboats.
03
Posterior Probability
The updated belief after combining prior and likelihood. After computing all features' Gaussian likelihoods and multiplying by the prior, we get the final probability of survival.
04
Naïve Assumption
The model assumes features are conditionally independent given the class. This is "naïve" because age and fare are correlated in reality — but GNB still achieves ~80% accuracy, proving the power of simplicity.
05
Gaussian Distribution
For continuous features like age and fare, we model P(feature | class) as a Gaussian bell curve parameterised by the mean and variance of that feature within each class, estimated from training data.
06
Log-Space Computation
Multiplying many small probabilities causes underflow. We work in log space: log P(A|B) = log P(A) + Σ log P(xᵢ|A), then use softmax to convert back. var_smoothing prevents log(0).

How Your Trained Model Works

Your Python pipeline does the following at prediction time:


1. Engineer features: family_size, is_alone, age_bin, fare_bin
2. StandardScaler: x_scaled = (x − μ) / σ (per training stats)
3. For each class c ∈ {0, 1}:
   log_prob[c] = log(prior[c])
   for each feature xᵢ:
     log_prob[c] += log( Gaussian(xᵢ; μ_ic, σ²_ic + var_smoothing) )
4. Predict class = argmax(log_prob)
5. Probability = softmax(log_prob)

Your GridSearchCV found the optimal var_smoothing over 100 log-spaced values from 10⁻¹² to 10⁻¹ using 10-fold cross validation.

What the EDA Revealed

Sex was the strongest predictor. Female passengers had a 74.2% survival rate vs. 18.9% for males — a direct result of the "women and children first" evacuation protocol.


Class mattered enormously. 1st class passengers had lifeboats physically closer to their cabins. 3rd class passengers were often physically blocked from reaching the upper decks.

Family size had a sweet spot. Travelling with a small family (2–4) improved survival. Large families (5+) had trouble evacuating together, reducing survival rates significantly.


Age binning outperformed raw age. Children (≤12) had elevated survival due to priority boarding. Your pd.cut into 5 bins captured this non-linear relationship.

ML Glossary

GaussianNB
A Naive Bayes classifier that models feature likelihoods as Gaussian distributions, parameterised by per-class mean and variance. Suitable for continuous features like age and fare.
from sklearn.naive_bayes import GaussianNB
var_smoothing
The key hyperparameter of GaussianNB. Adds a fraction of the largest feature variance to all variances, preventing numerical underflow. GridSearchCV found the optimal value across 100 candidates.
np.logspace(-12, -1, 100)
StandardScaler
Transforms features so each has zero mean and unit variance: x_scaled = (x − μ) / σ. Ensures numerical stability and helps GridSearchCV converge faster.
scaler = StandardScaler(); X_scaled = scaler.fit_transform(X)
Stratified Split
train_test_split with stratify=y ensures training and test sets have the same class ratio (38.4% survived) as the full dataset, preventing biased evaluation.
train_test_split(X, y, stratify=y, test_size=0.2)
ROC-AUC
Area Under the ROC Curve — measures how well the model separates the two classes across all decision thresholds. Your model achieved ~0.86 AUC, significantly above the 0.5 baseline.
LabelEncoder
Converts categorical strings to integers. sex: {female→0, male→1}. embarked: {C→0, Q→1, S→2}.
le = LabelEncoder(); df['sex'] = le.fit_transform(df['sex'])
10-Fold CV
Splits training data into 10 folds, trains on 9 and validates on 1, rotating through all folds. The mean accuracy is a more reliable estimate of generalisation performance.
